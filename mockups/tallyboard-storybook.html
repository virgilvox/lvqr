Tallyboard.

The console aesthetic for LVQR. A live broadcast-room feel for software that lives next to OBS, ffmpeg, and the rack of NVENC GPUs in the corner. Built around four ideas: paper not pixels, ink not lines, an amber tally light, and a cyan signal wire.

The four signals.

Every screen in LVQR resolves to a combination of these four pigments. Pick the wrong one and the page reads wrong. Bone is the room you're standing in. Ink is what's printed on the wall. Tally amber is the on-air light. Wire cyan is the signal moving through the cables.

Logo construction. The wordmark is set in Instrument Serif, weight 400, no kerning adjustments. The amber dot is exactly the cap-height divided by four, vertically aligned to baseline of the lowercase x-height. It always glows; the glow is box-shadow: 0 0 12px var(--tally-glow). Never replace the wordmark with a logomark; never separate the dot from the wordmark; never tint the dot anything other than tally amber.

The room vibe.

Tallyboard is meant to feel like the patch bay in a broadcast control room — 1U racks, vertical labels, color-coded signal paths, an on-air tally that's actually wired to something. Not skeumorphic. Just enough physical-world reference to make state legible at a glance from across the room.

When to use this system.

Tallyboard is the right system for dashboards, control surfaces, broadcast tooling, video infrastructure consoles, and any operator-facing UI where state legibility and density matter more than warmth. It is the wrong system for marketing pages, consumer products, blog posts, or anything aimed at end-viewers (audiences) rather than operators (engineers). For those, reach for hack.build punk zine or LumenCanvas paper-teal instead.

Color.

Every value is a CSS custom property. Never hardcode a hex. Never invent a shade. The full palette has fewer than two dozen values and that's the point.

Surfaces.

Light surfaces only. Tallyboard is a paper-tone system. There is no "dark mode" — the ink panels in the topbar and statusbar are not "dark mode," they are dark surfaces within the light document, the way a printed black bar runs across a white page. If you find yourself reaching for a #1a1a1a global background, you are reaching for the wrong design system.

Four signals beyond the brand pair, each with one specific job. Don't tint anything red unless something is on-air or broken; don't tint anything green unless something is working. State colors are a contract.

Don't. Use red for danger that isn't on-air. Use green for "selected." Use blue (any blue) anywhere — that's wire's job. Combine tally and wire in the same gradient. Stack three colored pills next to each other on the same row.
Do. Reserve on-air red for live signal and live signal only. Use chalk-lo as the "neutral idle" surface for a card that should not draw attention. Dim ink-faint or ink-ghost for any non-essential metadata. Treat tally amber as a budget — every screen gets one or two amber elements, not ten.

Type.

Three families. Instrument Serif for the editorial display voice. Figtree for the body. JetBrains Mono for everything that's a measurement, a label, or a piece of code. The mix is what makes the system feel like a console instead of a generic dashboard.

Instrument Serif.

Display only. Italic for emphasis. Used for page titles, KPI numerals, large card titles, brand wordmark. Never set body in this. Always pair an italic span (<em>) with the roman to anchor a head; the italic carries the editorial voice ("Tallyboard."). Period at the end of every page title. No exceptions.

Figtree.

Body and prose. 14px is the default body size; 13px works inside cards; 15px for the lead paragraph under a page title. Use 400 for body, 600 for sub-emphasis, 700 sparingly. Never set anything in Figtree larger than 18px — at that size you should be in Instrument Serif instead.

Borders & corners.

Square corners, always. Border-radius is zero across the entire system — there is no exception. Borders are 1px, almost always --chalk-hi, sometimes --ink for high-contrast emphasis. Cards use a 1px border, never a shadow. The shadow vocabulary is reserved for the rack-on-hover state and for the on-air glow.

Inline SVG only. 1.5px stroke. No fills (line icons), no two-tone, no emoji, no icon font. The icon style is engineering schematic — every icon should look like it could appear next to a switch on the back of a piece of broadcast gear.

Up isn't always good. The arrow direction is literal — "the metric went up" — not semantic. Use up-green only when the metric going up is desirable. Filter chain.

Per-fragment WebAssembly filters running in a wasmtime sandbox between ingest and fan-out. Hot-reload modules without dropping a frame. Chain them, attach them to broadcasts.

The italic word matters. Pick the one word in the title that carries the metaphor. "Filter chain" — the chain is the verb. "Stream detail" — the detail is the focus. "Tallyboard overview" — the board is the object. The italic anchors the page emotionally; without it, headlines feel mechanical.

Voice & tone.

Tallyboard sounds like an operator who has already done this a thousand times and assumes you have too. Direct, technical, comfortable with jargon, occasionally dry. Not warm, not chatty, not "delightful." This is software for engineers running broadcast infrastructure.

Page titles.

do. "Filter chain." · "Tallyboard overview." · "DVR scrubber." · "Cross-cluster federation." · "Egress endpoints."
don't. "Welcome to your filters!" · "Manage Recordings" · "View Cluster Details" · "Streams Dashboard" · titles without periods.
The pattern. One or two words, ending in a period, with the second word italicized. Reads like a chapter heading in a manual. The italic anchors the metaphor; the period closes the thought. Avoid verb-led titles ("Manage X," "View Y") — those are CMS chrome, not console chrome.

Lead paragraphs.

do. "Per-fragment WebAssembly filters running in a wasmtime sandbox between ingest and fan-out. Drop in a .wasm module, hot-reload it without dropping a frame, chain filters into a pipeline."
don't. "Welcome! Filters let you customize your stream by adding effects and transformations. Get started by clicking the New Filter button below to upload your first filter."
Address the operator, not the new user. Lead paragraphs explain what the system does, not how to use it. They use the actual technical names of things (wasmtime, fragment, fan-out) instead of metaphors. They refer to specific implementations the operator can reason about. They never welcome anyone.

House rules.

never. Em-dashes (—) anywhere in any output. Em-dashes are an LLM tic. Use a colon, semicolon, period, or just two sentences instead.
never. Emojis anywhere. Inline SVG icons only.
never. "Not just X, it's Y." "Whether you're A or B…" Other AI-tinged framing patterns. They're cliches and they erode the operator voice.
always. Use mono for any measurement, identifier, or piece of code, even inline. Use specific numbers ("142ms p95") rather than vague ones ("low latency"). Use "broadcast" instead of "stream" when referring to a logical channel; "stream" refers to the actual data flow.

Side-by-side.

do. "Federation forwards broadcasts across LVQR clusters over a single authenticated QUIC link. Push or pull, config-driven, with broadcast-name patterns and ACL."
don't. "Federation is a powerful feature that lets you connect multiple LVQR servers together — whether you're scaling globally or partnering with another cluster, federation has you covered."
